# VJ Practitioner Interview: Charles

**Source:** Interview by Seth Drew, April 2026
**Subject:** Charles — VJ, media producer, visual performer
**Format:** Semi-structured interview, edited from raw notes

---

## Background

Charles comes from media production and storytelling — video, print, live and non-live formats. His path to VJing was through a personal interest in using creativity to access mindful states: plant medicine ceremonies, sober psychonaut practices, personal growth work. He describes a "longing for creative expression" rooted in connecting with yourself, not in technical mastery for its own sake.

His entry point was video as a background medium for audio experiences. A friend was doing old-school video synthesis — dripping oil onto a plate and projecting it onto a screen. This planted the idea of marrying audio and visual in real time.

## Creative Philosophy: Discovery vs. Control

Charles frames his VJ practice around a central question: **"How do I operate in the intersection between discovery and control?"**

The goal, as he describes it, is to minimize the lag between intention and output — "how quickly can I get my mind out of the way and uplink to the source directly." He wants to reduce the cognitive distance between what he feels and what appears on screen, editing from within a flow state rather than making deliberate analytical decisions.

His personal preparation process illustrates this:

1. Meditate on the heart
2. Make audio from that state
3. Meditate to the audio
4. Make visuals while meditating, projecting the audio/video onto himself

This is a calibration loop — each stage tunes him deeper into the material before he performs with it.

### The Memory Palace Setup

Charles's pre-performance preparation is what makes real-time flow possible. His setup consists of **samples and the ability to manipulate those samples** — a curated toolkit he's internalized deeply enough to operate without deliberation. He describes this as a "memory palace": the creative vocabulary is pre-loaded so that performance becomes navigation, not construction.

> "Sort of like jazz."

He notes that some performers are "naturals" who uplink to this flow state without the preparation work. For most, the pre-work is what enables it.

## Live Interactivity Model

### Ear-to-Hand Over Auto-Reactivity

Charles's default mode is **ear-to-hand control**: he listens and responds manually, with audio reactivity as a secondary layer rather than the primary driver. "Put away the reactivity, control by hand."

When he does use audio reactivity, **BPM-reactive patterns are the primary tool** — for example, a saw wave pattern locked to tempo. More granular audio analysis (mapping specific spectral features to visual parameters) is secondary and handled through tools like TouchDesigner or Hydra.

His signal chain for shows:
- **TouchDesigner or Hydra** — map audio qualities to visual parameters (movement, color, particle behavior within frequency ranges)
- **Resolume** — map content to LED walls or projection surfaces

### Dimensions of Control

For live performance, Charles sets up **1 to 4 desired dimensions of control**. More than that and it becomes unwieldy. The three primary dimensions he works with:

| Dimension | Description | Control model |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Image composition** | Things fading in or layering on top of one another | Point A to Point B (crossfade) |
| **Color** | Palette, tone, mood | Point A to Point B |
| **Movement / speed** | Animation rate, direction, turbulence | Continuous |

Each dimension is a simple A-to-B continuum or a continuous knob — not a complex parameter space.

### Audio Reactivity Parameters

When audio reactivity is active, he works with two key controls:

- **Gain** — how much audio signal to take in (in dB). Controls sensitivity/threshold.
- **Fall** — how quickly the pulse holds before returning to zero. Controls the decay envelope of the reactive response. Measured in time (seconds).

These two parameters — essentially attack/release or sensitivity/decay — are the entire audio reactivity interface. This maps directly to what our system calls normalization time constants and thresholds.

### Physical Interface

Charles's minimal hardware setup is a **Korg nanoKONTROL** — a small MIDI controller with sliders and knobs. Key observations about physical controls:

- Sliders and knobs are the right form factor
- **Knobs can be infinite** (continuous rotation) — important for dimensions without hard endpoints
- He's interested in **non-traditional input devices** and **haptic information** as future directions

## Design Principles for Interactive Visual Tools

Charles has a film production and storytelling background, not an engineering one. This gives him a different frame for MVP:

> "Maximum impact/feeling by way of simplest setup."

His recommendations for tool design:

### Make It Feel Like Magic
- The goal is **"magic whimsy, feels intuitive"**
- If it's overly complex, people lose interest
- **Make them feel like an expert** — the tool should make the user feel capable, not overwhelmed
- The speed of experience for a new user needs to be **much slower** than what a power user expects

### Chaos and Randomness Are Features
- Some degree of chaos or randomness is essential
- Under the influence of substances: "Holy fuck, it's syncing up" — the moment of apparent synchronization is powerful precisely because it emerges from partial randomness
- **"Humans are smoothing machines"** — people will perceive pattern and intention in stochastic processes. You don't need to engineer perfect sync; approximate sync + human pattern-matching = perceived magic
- Algo raves exist as a subculture where the audience understands and appreciates the generative/algorithmic nature of what they're seeing

## Tangential Notes

### Pixel Art and Display
- Blocky pixel art was **designed to be run through CRT televisions** — the CRT's phosphor bloom and scanline behavior completed the image
- Running LED pixels through a screen or clear lens sheet could **reduce flooding** (harsh point-source look)
- Dimensional framing: "We don't live in a 3D world — we live in a 4D world being projected into 2D space. With time you're creating a 3D simulation in front of you." The implication: still operating with a 2D visual goal but projecting into different dimensions

### Hardware Scavenging
- Disposable vapes contain usable battery cells with charging circuits — potential power source for portable LED projects

## Key Takeaways for the LED System

1. **Ear-to-hand is the VJ default.** Audio reactivity is a layer, not the foundation. Our automated system is doing something VJs deliberately avoid — which means we need to be better than a human at the reactive layer to justify removing human control.

2. **1-4 dimensions of control, no more.** Effect design should expose a small number of meaningful axes, not a parameter dump. Each axis should feel like a simple A-to-B journey.

3. **Gain and fall are the whole audio reactivity interface.** Sensitivity and decay time. Our normalization system is essentially implementing these two knobs — this validates the approach.

4. **Humans are smoothing machines.** We may be over-engineering sync precision. Approximate correlation + randomness may produce better perceived results than tight analytical mapping.

5. **Pre-work enables flow.** The "memory palace" concept — deeply internalizing a curated toolkit — suggests that effect presets and parameter ranges should be carefully curated rather than exhaustively configurable.

6. **Film production MVP: maximum feeling, simplest setup.** Complexity is the enemy. A show that feels magical with 2 parameters beats one that's technically impressive with 20.
